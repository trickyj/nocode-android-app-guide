Phase 0
Hardware & Devices You Need
Must Have
```
A Computer or Laptop
Windows, Mac, or Linux — all work. Minimum: 4GB RAM, 50GB free space, decent internet. Recommended: 8GB RAM. You'll use this for everything — designing, building, uploading.
Required
An Android Phone
Any Android phone running Android 8.0 (Oreo) or higher. You'll use this to test your app on a real device. Don't have one? You can use a free online emulator, but a real phone is better.
Required
Stable Internet Connection
Minimum 5 Mbps. You'll be using cloud-based no-code tools that run in your browser — good internet = smooth experience.
Required
A USB Cable (Phone to Computer)
To transfer your APK file to your phone for testing. Most Android charging cables work as data cables too.
Required
```

Phase 1
Accounts & Registrations to Set Up
Mostly Free
```
Google Account (Gmail)
You need a Google account for everything — Google Play Console, Firebase, Drive. Create one at gmail.com if you don't have one already. Use your personal email, not college email.
Free
Google Play Developer Account
Go to play.google.com/console → sign in → pay a one-time $25 registration fee (~₹2,100). This unlocks the ability to publish apps forever. You'll need a debit/credit card. Split cost with friends if needed!
$25 One-Time
No-Code App Builder Account (Choose One)
Create a free account on your chosen builder — MIT App Inventor (free), Thunkable (free tier), FlutterFlow (free tier), or Kodular (free). Details in Phase 2 below.
Free
Canva Account (for App Graphics)
You'll need to create your app icon (512×512px), feature graphic (1024×500px), and screenshots. Canva.com is free and has templates for all of these.
Free
Firebase Account (for Backend/Database — Optional)
If your app needs user login, a database, or push notifications — create a free Firebase account at firebase.google.com. Uses your Google account. Free tier is generous for student projects.
Optional
💡 Tip on $25: The Google Play Developer fee is a one-time payment per Google account. 3–4 friends can pool it, publish all their apps under one developer account, and share the cost!
```

Phase 3
Plan Your App Before Building
Do This First
```
Define Your App Idea in One Sentence
Example: "An app that shows today's college canteen menu" or "A quiz app for my class." Keep it simple. First app should solve ONE small problem. Don't try to build Instagram.
List the Screens Your App Needs
Write on paper: Home Screen, Menu Screen, About Screen, etc. Most simple apps need only 3–5 screens. This is called a "wireframe" — just boxes and labels, no design needed yet.
Decide What Your App Does (Features List)
Example features: Show a list of items → Let user tap to see details → Add a search bar. Limit yourself to 3–5 features for Version 1. You can always add more later.
Choose Your App Category
Will it be Education, Tools, Entertainment, Health, or Social? You'll need this when uploading to Play Store. Pick the most relevant category.
Think About Who Will Use It
Your target audience — students? local shopkeepers? fitness lovers? This affects your app's design and what Play Store description you'll write.
⚠️ Most common beginner mistake: Trying to build too much in the first version. Build a tiny V1, publish it, get feedback, then improve. Shipping beats perfection.
```
🔨
Phase 4
Build & Test Your App
Core Phase
```
Watch the Official Tutorial for Your Builder
Before touching anything, watch the official "Getting Started" video on YouTube for your chosen tool. MIT App Inventor, Thunkable, and Kodular all have beginner playlists. 1–2 hours of watching saves you days of confusion.
Create Your First Screen — The Home Screen
Drag and drop a label, a button, and maybe an image. Get comfortable with adding components. Change colors and font sizes. Don't worry about it looking perfect yet.
Add Functionality Using Blocks/Logic
Every no-code tool has a "logic" or "blocks" section. This is where you say "When user taps button → go to next screen." Connect your buttons and actions here step by step.
Enable Developer Mode on Your Android Phone
Go to: Settings → About Phone → tap "Build Number" 7 times → Developer Options appear → Enable "USB Debugging." This lets your computer talk to your phone for testing.
Key Step
Test Your App on Real Device While Building
MIT App Inventor has a companion app — install "MIT AI2 Companion" from Play Store. Scan QR code in App Inventor to preview live on your phone. Test every button, every screen, every feature.
Get 3–5 Friends to Test Your App (Beta Test)
Share the APK with friends. Ask: Does it crash? Is anything confusing? Fix the bugs they find before submitting to Play Store. This is called "user testing."
Don't Skip
Export Your App as an APK or AAB File
In your builder, look for "Export → Download APK" or "Build → Android App Bundle (.aab)". Play Store now prefers .aab files. APK is for direct sharing/testing. Save this file safely on your computer.
Key Step
```

Phase 5
Prepare Your Play Store Assets
Before Submitting
```
App Icon — 512 × 512 px PNG
Design in Canva. Simple, colorful, recognizable. No text in icon ideally. Export as PNG with transparent background if possible. This is the icon users will see on Play Store and on their home screen.
Required
Feature Graphic — 1024 × 500 px
This banner appears at the top of your Play Store listing. Add your app name + a tagline + a nice background. Canva has ready-made templates for this size.
Required
Screenshots — Minimum 2, Best to Have 4–6
Take screenshots of your app on your phone. Play Store needs phone screenshots (portrait). Use Canva to add a frame and short caption above each screenshot. Shows users what the app looks like before installing.
Required
Write a Short App Description (Max 80 chars)
This appears below your app name. Example: "Find today's canteen menu — updated daily!" Be honest and clear. No fake claims.
Required
Write a Full Description (up to 4,000 chars)
Explain: What does the app do? Who is it for? Key features (use bullet points). How to use it. You can use ChatGPT to help write this — just paste it in and edit it to sound natural.
Required
Create a Privacy Policy Page
Google requires ALL apps to have a privacy policy — even simple ones. Use a free generator: privacypolicygenerator.info or app-privacy-policy.com. Host it on Google Sites (free) or GitHub Pages and paste the link in Play Console.
Required
🎯 Canva shortcut: Search "App Store Screenshot" in Canva templates — there are hundreds of professional-looking templates you can customize in minutes. Makes your Play Store listing look 10× more polished.
```
🚀
Phase 6
Upload to Google Play Store
$25 One-Time
```
Go to play.google.com/console and Sign In
Login with your Google account. If you haven't paid the $25 fee yet, do it now. Once paid, your developer account is active forever.
Click "Create App" and Fill in Basic Info
Select: App or Game → Free or Paid → Default language → App Name. Then click Create. You'll now see the app dashboard with a checklist Google gives you.
Fill the "Store Listing" Section
Upload your icon, feature graphic, screenshots, short description, full description, category, tags, contact email, and privacy policy URL. Take your time — this is what users see.
Key Step
Upload Your AAB File Under "Releases"
Go to Production → Create New Release → Upload your .aab file. Give the release a name (e.g., "v1.0 Initial Launch"). Add release notes like "First version — try it and give us feedback!"
Key Step
Complete the Content Rating Questionnaire
Google asks what kind of content is in your app (violence? adult themes? location data?). Answer honestly. For most student apps, everything is "No." You'll get a rating like "Everyone" or "Teen."
Required
Set Pricing & Distribution
Select "Free" (recommended for first app). Choose which countries to publish in — start with India, expand later. Check the boxes for distribution countries.
Review All Warnings → Submit for Review
Google Play Console will show a checklist. Fix everything marked red or yellow. When it's all green — click "Send for Review." Google reviews apps in 1–7 days for new accounts.
Final Step
Wait for Approval & Go Live! 🎉
Google will email you when your app is approved (usually 3–7 days). Once approved, search for your app on Play Store — and share the link with everyone you know. You're officially an app developer!
Celebrate!
⏳ First-time accounts take longer: New developer accounts often wait 3–7 days for review. Don't panic if it takes time. If rejected, read Google's reason carefully — most rejections are easy to fix (missing privacy policy, bad screenshots, etc.).
```
